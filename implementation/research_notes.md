## Question 1 — Louisiana Commission Rebate Legality (highest priority)

> **This is research, not legal advice. Final word = Carrie's eXp broker/compliance team and/or LREC (or a Louisiana-licensed real estate attorney).**

### Bottom line

**Buyer-side commission rebates are legal in Louisiana** — and have been since **Louisiana Attorney General Opinion 21-0030 (Oct. 26, 2021)**, which was requested by LREC's own counsel and read directly (primary source PDF). Its holding, quoted from the cover page:

> "Neither La. R.S. 37:1445, 1446, nor 1455(A)(7) prohibit a licensee of the Louisiana Real Estate Commission from rebating a portion of the licensee's commission to a real estate consumer." `[high]`

Reasoning: §§37:1445/1446 restrict paying *compensation* to *unlicensed persons* for real estate *activity* — a rebate to your own client is a discount/refund, not "compensation," and a consumer acting as principal in their own deal isn't performing "real estate activity." §37:1455(A)(7) disciplines only **undisclosed** rebates — "undisclosed" is the operative word, so **disclosed rebates are affirmatively fine**. The opinion anchors this to NAR Code of Ethics Article 6: disclosure/consent is the condition, not a ban. `[high — primary source]`

**A discounted LISTING commission (Carrie's fee when Brook sells his current home) is unambiguously fine.** It isn't a "rebate" at all in the legal sense — it's an agent negotiating her own fee with her own client in the listing agreement. No unlicensed-person issue, no undisclosed-payment issue; no US state restricts an agent from simply charging less. Cleanest structure by a wide margin. `[high]`

### What's prohibited / restricted / clean

| Structure | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Discounted listing commission (Brook's sale) | **Clean** | Just a negotiated fee in the listing agreement. Only wrinkle: eXp's split economics fall on Carrie. |
| Buyer-side rebate, disclosed + on closing paperwork | **Legal with conditions** | See conditions below. Blessed by AG Opinion 21-0030. |
| Undisclosed rebate / off-the-books post-closing check | **Risky — avoid** | This is the exact fact pattern §37:1455(A)(7) disciplines ("undisclosed commission, rebate, or profit"), and hiding a credit from a lender creates separate mortgage-side risk. |
| Rebate/fee to an unlicensed third party for a referral | **Restricted (§37:1446(A))** | Not relevant here — Brook would be Carrie's direct, disclosed client. |

### Conditions on a buyer-side rebate

1. **Must be disclosed** (written disclosure is best practice — the statutory discipline trigger is non-disclosure). `[high]`
2. **Louisiana now requires a written Buyer Representation Agreement** before a broker shows property to a buyer client (Act 690, 2024 Regular Session, eff. 8/19/2024), stating compensation "in a manner that is objectively ascertainable and not open-ended." This is the natural, legally required vehicle to document any rebate arrangement with Brook up front. `[medium-high]`
3. **If Brook finances the purchase**, the rebate should be written into the offer and shown as a credit on the Closing Disclosure — not paid as a personal check afterward. Lenders must see it (cash-to-close, concession limits). Under longstanding HUD/RESPA interpretation, an agent rebating her *own* commission to her *own* client is not a RESPA §8 kickback — but it still needs the paper trail. `[medium]`

### eXp Realty angle

Not confirmed from official eXp policy docs (third-party agent blogs only, low-medium confidence): eXp's 80/20 split to a $16k annual cap, with a ~$500 minimum "company dollar" per transaction side, means **Carrie absorbs eXp's cut regardless of what she gives Brook**, and the arrangement flows through eXp's transaction paperwork/broker review. Nothing suggests eXp blocks discounts/rebates, but Carrie should loop in her eXp team lead/broker before finalizing numbers — for reconciliation and residual-compliance sign-off, especially given the new (2024) buyer-agreement disclosure landscape. `[low-medium]`

### Recommended structures, ranked by legal cleanliness

1. **Discounted listing commission when Carrie lists Brook's current home** — cleanest; no rebate-law question even arises.
2. **Buyer-side commission rebate credited at closing**, documented in the written Buyer Representation Agreement and shown on the Closing Disclosure — legal, well-trodden, low risk if disclosed; slightly more procedural friction (transaction paperwork + lender if financed).
3. **Post-closing personal payment/gift off the paperwork — avoid.** If it's consideration for the transaction but kept off the purchase agreement/CD, it's the "undisclosed rebate" fact pattern the statute actually disciplines, plus lender-disclosure risk.

### Discrepancy flagged

Some secondary sources (notably CAARE's "States That Prohibit Realtor Fee Negotiations" page and mirrors) **still list Louisiana among 10 rebate-ban states — this is stale content predating the Oct 2021 AG opinion**. Every dated, event-specific source (AG opinion itself; CFA press release 12/15/2021; REX PR 12/14/2021) agrees Louisiana came off the ban list in late 2021, leaving nine ban states (AL, AK, IA, KS, MS, MO, OK, OR, TN). The DOJ's own justice.gov/atr pages could not be fetched (403/SSL errors), so DOJ's current page status for Louisiana is unverified either way. `[high for the correction; low/unverified for the current DOJ page text]`
