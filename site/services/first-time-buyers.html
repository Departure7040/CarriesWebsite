The Basics

The first-time buyer process, step by step

1. Get pre-approved first

Pre-approval means a lender has checked your income, debt and credit and told you what you can actually borrow. It comes before house-hunting, not after — without it, sellers won't take your offer seriously, and you risk falling for a home that's out of reach.

2. Set a realistic budget

Your budget should account for the full monthly payment (principal, interest, taxes, insurance) — not just the sale price. Down Payments & Loan Programs

Down payment and loan programs to know about

Most first-time buyers don't put 20% down — several loan programs allow much less, and Louisiana has first-time-buyer assistance options worth asking about. Program names, eligibility rules and down-payment minimums change over time, so treat the items below as a starting point for a conversation, not final terms.

Programs worth asking Carrie about Sample — Carrie confirms

What: FHA loans (lower down payment, more flexible credit requirements), VA loans (for eligible veterans and service members), USDA loans (for eligible rural and some Acadiana-area properties), and Louisiana Housing Corporation first-time-buyer assistance programs are all commonly discussed with first-time buyers.

Why it matters: The right program can significantly lower what you need up front — but eligibility rules and terms change, and getting this wrong is a real financial risk.

What to do: Don't treat this list as final terms. Ask Carrie and a local lender to confirm current eligibility and numbers for your specific situation before you budget around any one program.

Avoid These

Common first-time buyer mistakes

Shopping before pre-approval

Falling for a home before you know your real budget leads to disappointment — or an offer that isn't taken seriously.

Waiving the inspection

In a competitive market it's tempting to skip it to look stronger. An inspection is one of the best ways to avoid an expensive surprise after closing.

Underestimating closing costs

Beyond the down payment, budget for closing costs, moving expenses and an emergency cushion for the first few months.

Making a big financial change mid-process

New credit cards, a new car, or a job change between pre-approval and closing can jeopardize your loan. Keep your finances steady until keys are in hand.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

How much down payment do I actually need?

It depends on the loan program — some options allow well under 20% down. Exact minimums change with the program and your qualification, so Carrie walks through current options with you rather than quoting one number for everyone.

What credit score do I need to buy a house?

There's no single cutoff — it varies by loan type, and a lender can tell you exactly where you stand. Working on your credit before you shop is still one of the most useful first steps.

What are closing costs and who pays them?

Closing costs are the fees and prepaid items due at closing, separate from your down payment. Who pays what is often negotiable as part of the offer, which Carrie will walk you through.

Is it cheaper to rent or buy in Lafayette right now?

It depends on current rates, home prices, and how long you plan to stay — there's no one-size-fits-all answer. Carrie can walk through the numbers for your specific situation.

How long does the whole process take from search to closing?

Timelines vary by buyer and market conditions. Carrie will give you a realistic estimate for your situation once she knows what you're looking for.

Do I need a real estate agent as a first-time buyer, or can I go it alone?

You're not required to use an agent, but a buyer's agent works on your side of the deal — helping with pricing, negotiation, and catching things a first-timer might miss — at no direct cost that isn't spelled out in your buyer-agency agreement up front.
