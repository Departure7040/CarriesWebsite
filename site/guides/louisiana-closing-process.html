How Closing on a Home Works in Louisiana

If you've bought a home anywhere else in the U.S., Louisiana's closing process will feel unfamiliar — there's no traditional escrow, and a notary plays the role your title or escrow company played elsewhere. Here's the plain-language version of how it actually works, and where Carrie fits in to walk you through it.

Why Louisiana Feels Different

Louisiana isn't an escrow state — and it isn't common law, either

Louisiana is the only U.S. state that operates under a civil law system, derived from the Napoleonic Code and French and Spanish colonial law, rather than the English common law system used by the other 49 states (per Quill Title & Closing). That's not a technicality — it's the reason closing here doesn't look like closing in Texas, Georgia, or wherever you're relocating from. Instead of an escrow-held closing process, Louisiana property transfers happen through a document called an act of sale, executed in front of a notary.

The Louisiana Equivalent of "Closing"

What an act of sale actually is

An act of sale is Louisiana's equivalent of a closing: a formal, notarized document that transfers property ownership from seller to buyer under Civil Code Article 1833 (per Quill Title & Closing). Under that same article, an authentic act — including an act of sale — must be executed before a notary public in the presence of two witnesses, and signed by each party, each witness, and the notary (per Justia). Louisiana law requires this notary-and-two-witnesses process for all property sales statewide (per NOLA Title Authority).

Louisiana notaries come in two types: attorney-notaries, who are licensed attorneys holding a notary commission, and non-attorney notaries, who are commissioned by the governor after passing a notary examination (per Quill Title & Closing). Either type can conduct your act of sale — but for title insurance purposes, an attorney must still examine the title and issue a title opinion even when a non-attorney notary handles the signing (per Quill Title & Closing).

The Sequence

From offer to act of sale: the general timeline

Louisiana home closings typically take 30 to 60 days from an executed purchase agreement to the act of sale (per Quill Title & Closing). Here's roughly how that window breaks down:

1. Purchase agreement signed

REALTOR® members statewide typically use the standard residential purchase agreement published by the Louisiana Association of REALTORS (LAR), which includes provisions specific to Louisiana's civil law system (per LAR).

2. Earnest money deposited

Earnest money is typically deposited within 24 hours of contract ratification (per Quill Title & Closing).

3. Attorney title examination

An attorney examines the title, typically 14 to 30 days after the purchase agreement is executed — a critical step that surfaces any liens or ownership issues before closing (per Quill Title & Closing).

4. Act of sale executed

Buyer, seller, two witnesses and the notary sign the act of sale, transferring ownership under Civil Code Article 1833 (per Justia).

5. Act of sale recorded

The notary must record the act of sale with the parish Clerk of Court within 15 days outside Orleans Parish, or within 48 hours inside Orleans Parish (per Louisiana Legislature).

6. Transfer is final

Once the act of sale is recorded with the parish Clerk of Court, the property transfer is effective against third parties (per Quill Title & Closing).

Earnest money works differently here too

What: Under Louisiana Civil Code Article 2624, if a seller recedes (backs out) without a contractual cause, the seller must return the buyer's earnest money plus an equal amount — the "double-return" rule (per Quill Title & Closing).

Why it matters: Relocators from escrow states often expect earnest money disputes to work the way they do elsewhere. Louisiana's civil-code remedy is different, and it's worth understanding before you sign.

What to do: Ask Carrie to walk through your specific purchase agreement's earnest money terms, and confirm anything contract-specific with your closing attorney or notary.

What It Costs

Typical closing costs in Louisiana

Costs vary by parish, lender, and the specifics of your transaction, but as a general benchmark:

Buyers

Buyers in Louisiana typically pay 2% to 5% of the home's purchase price in closing costs (per Southern Title).

Sellers

Average seller closing costs in Louisiana are approximately 3.15% of the purchase price (per Clever Real Estate).

These are broad percentage benchmarks, not itemized quotes — the specific line items (notary fees, recording fees, title work, inspections and more) vary by transaction and parish. Ask Carrie and your title professional for an itemized estimate before you budget (confirm with a professional).

Local Variation

Recording happens at the parish level

Louisiana has 64 parishes, each with its own Clerk of Court office that handles property recording — and recording fees, procedures and turnaround times vary by parish (per Quill Title & Closing). That means the exact recording timeline and fees for your closing depend on which parish the property sits in. Lafayette Parish-specific recording timelines beyond the general statewide pattern above — confirm with a professional (your notary or the Lafayette Parish Clerk of Court) for your transaction.

Protecting Your Purchase

Is title insurance required in Louisiana?

Title insurance is not required for buyers in Louisiana, but it's highly recommended — and lenders always require a mortgagee title insurance policy as security for their loan, so if you're financing, some form of title insurance is effectively part of the deal (per Crescent Title).

Married buyers: community property matters Confirm with a professional

What: Louisiana presumes that property owned by a married person is community property, with exceptions for gifts, inheritances, and property acquired before marriage (per Louisiana Law Help). Community property generally includes property acquired during the marriage through either spouse's effort or skill, and property donated to both spouses jointly (per Vernon Parish Chamber of Commerce). Both spouses generally must concur in the sale, mortgage, or lease of community real estate — one spouse typically cannot unilaterally sell or mortgage a shared family home (per Louisiana Law Help).

Why it matters: If you're married and relocating to buy in Louisiana, this can affect how title is held and whose signature is required at the act of sale.

What to do: This is exactly the kind of question to raise with your closing attorney or notary early — how you and your spouse want to hold title affects the paperwork from day one. (Confirm with a professional; this is general information, not legal advice for your situation.)

FAQ

Common questions about Louisiana closings

Why is Louisiana different from every other state in how home closings work?

Louisiana is the only U.S. state built on a civil law system, derived from the Napoleonic Code and French and Spanish colonial law, rather than the English common law used everywhere else. That's why closing happens through a notarized "act of sale" rather than a traditional escrow closing (per Quill Title & Closing).

What is an act of sale, and how is it different from a traditional closing or warranty deed?

An act of sale is Louisiana's equivalent of a closing — a formal, notarized document, executed before a notary public and two witnesses, that transfers ownership under Civil Code Article 1833 (per Quill Title & Closing; Justia).

Do you need an attorney for a Louisiana home closing?

Not necessarily to sign — a non-attorney notary can conduct the act of sale. But for title insurance purposes, an attorney must still examine the title and issue a title opinion, even when a non-attorney notary handles the signing (per Quill Title & Closing). Whether you personally want an attorney involved beyond that is worth discussing with a professional for your specific transaction (confirm with a professional).

How does Louisiana's earnest money system work differently from other states?

Earnest money is typically deposited within 24 hours of contract ratification, and under Civil Code Article 2624, a seller who backs out without contractual cause must return the earnest money plus an equal amount — the "double-return" rule (per Quill Title & Closing).

What is the typical timeline from making an offer to closing on a home in Louisiana?

Generally 30 to 60 days from an executed purchase agreement to the act of sale, with attorney title examination typically happening 14 to 30 days in (per Quill Title & Closing).

How does community property affect a married couple's home purchase in Louisiana?

Property owned by a married person is presumed to be community property, and both spouses generally must concur in the sale, mortgage, or lease of community real estate (per Louisiana Law Help). Who records the act of sale, and where?

The notary records the act of sale with the parish Clerk of Court — Louisiana has 64 parishes, each with its own Clerk of Court office, recording fees and procedures. The notary must record within 15 days outside Orleans Parish, or 48 hours inside Orleans Parish (per Louisiana Legislature; Quill Title & Closing).

What are typical closing cost categories for Louisiana buyers and sellers?
