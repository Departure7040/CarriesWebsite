The Hard Part

The real challenge: buying and selling at the same time

Move-up buyers face a puzzle first-timers never do: your down payment is usually locked inside the home you haven't sold yet. There's no single right answer — each path trades money for certainty in a different way, and which one fits depends on your finances and your tolerance for moving twice:

Path 1 — Sell first, then buy

The financially safest route: your equity is cash in hand and your offer on the next home is stronger. The trade-off is the gap — you may need short-term housing, or to negotiate staying in your sold home briefly after closing where the buyer agrees. Terms like that are negotiated case by case.

Path 2 — Buy contingent on your sale

Your offer on the new home includes a contingency: the purchase only goes through if your current home sells. It protects you from owning two houses, but it can make your offer less attractive when a seller is comparing it against non-contingent ones — pricing your current home realistically becomes the whole ballgame.

Path 3 — Buy first, then sell

Cleanest move, biggest financial lift: you generally need to qualify carrying both homes, or use financing tools your lender can walk you through. Worth discussing with a lender before assuming it's out of reach — and before assuming it's safe.

This is general education, not financial advice — the right sequencing depends on your equity, income and lender's requirements. Carrie coordinates both sides of the timeline; your lender confirms what the numbers allow.

Trade-Offs

Schools, space, commute: the move-up triangle

In this band you can usually get two of the three things families want — top school proximity, a bigger lot, a short commute — but rarely all three at the same price. New construction in a growing town often means more square footage but smaller lots; established neighborhoods flip that. There's no universal answer, only your ranking of the three.

FAQ

Move-up questions Carrie hears most

Should I list my current home before we start touring?

Usually you should at least know its realistic market value first — that number sets your whole budget. Whether you list before touring depends on which of the three timing paths fits you; Carrie typically starts with a valuation conversation, not a listing agreement.

Will sellers take my offer seriously if it's contingent on my home selling?

It depends on the market and the listing — a well-priced contingent offer with your home already listed (or under contract) reads very differently from one where your house isn't even on the market yet. Preparation is most of the answer.

Can Carrie handle both the sale and the purchase?

Yes — that's the core of her move-up work. Representing you on both transactions lets the two timelines be planned together instead of colliding by accident.

What if my home sells before we find the next one?

That scenario is planned for up front: options can include negotiating a longer closing, arranging to remain in the home briefly after closing if the buyer agrees, or short-term housing. Knowing your fallback before you list keeps a fast sale from becoming a crisis.

Is new construction or an existing home the better move-up buy?

Neither, universally. New construction offers warranties and modern layouts; established homes often offer bigger lots, mature neighborhoods and negotiating room. In this band around Youngsville, Broussard and south Lafayette you'll genuinely have both options to compare.
