Your Google Business Profile exists — and it's strong

Both are fixable — carefully, and only after confirming the approach with Carrie — but this profile should be protected and optimized first, not rebuilt.

Why It Matters

What this does to rankings

Google's local ranking system leans heavily on matching your name, address, and phone number (NAP) the same way across the web to build confidence that all these listings describe the same real business. When it finds six different addresses tied to "Carrie Billeaud, Realtor," it can't merge them into one clearly trusted entity with full confidence — and that uncertainty shows up as weaker placement in local map-pack results, the exact spot where most "realtor near me" searches get answered.

The same problem compounds for AI tools: chat-based search products build their answers by cross-referencing exactly the kind of public profile data in the table above. Inconsistent NAP data doesn't just confuse Google — it makes Carrie a less confident answer for ChatGPT, Perplexity, and similar tools too. And it isn't only a machine problem: a prospective client double-checking a phone number before calling, and landing on the wrong one, is a lost lead in a very ordinary, human way.

Fixing This

The fix, in short

  1. Protect the Google Business Profile first. Fix the keyword-decorated name carefully (with Carrie's sign-off), clean up the tracked website link, and complete categories/services/photos — this is already the strongest asset, so the priority is protecting it, not rebuilding it.
  2. Pick one canonical name, phone, address, and email and update every profile to match it — a short decision from Carrie, then a cleanup pass across all 12 sources.
  3. Resolve the ICON Agent multiplier and other career-metric discrepancies with Carrie before they're used anywhere in new marketing copy.
